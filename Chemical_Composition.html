Chemical Composition

Gasoline is a complex mixture of hydrocarbons and other compounds. Its chemical composition can vary based on the source of crude oil, refining processes, and the specific formulation for different grades. Here are the primary components typically found in gasoline:

Alkanes

Alkanes are organic compounds that consist entirely of single-bonded carbon and hydrogen atoms and lack any other functional groups. Gasoline primarily consists of a variety of hydrocarbons, particularly alkanes (also known as paraffins). Alkanes have the general formula CnH2n+2 and can be subdivided into the following three groups: the linear straight-chain alkanes, branched alkanes, and cycloalkanes.

Common Alkanes in Gasoline

› Pentanes (C5H12)

is present in volatile petroleum fractions and is used as a fuel; in the production of ammonia, olefin, and hydrogen. It also contributes to the gasoline fuel's volatility.

› Hexanes (C6H14)

n-Hexane is a chemical made from crude oil. Pure n-Hexane is a colorless liquid with a slightly disagreeable odor. It is highly flammable, and its vapors can be explosive. Several consumer products contain n-Hexane, such as gasoline, quick-drying glues used in various hobbies, and rubber cement.

› Heptane (C7H16)

N-heptane is a clear colorless liquid with a petroleum-like odor. Flash point 25 °F. Less dense than water and insoluble in water. Vapors heavier than air. Heptane is often used as a measure of the octane rating of gasoline.

› Octane (C8H18)

is a colorless liquid with an odor of gasoline. Octane is crucial for determining fuel quality; higher octane ratings mean better performance and less knocking.

› Nonane (C9H20)

N-nonane appears as a clear colorless liquid with a sharp odor and is present in small quantities, contributing to the overall energy content of gasoline.

› Decane (C10H22)

Also appears as a colorless liquid which is also found in gasoline. Decane plays a role in the fuel's combustion characteristics.

Alkenes

Alkanes are organic compounds that consist entirely of single-bonded carbon and hydrogen atoms and lack any other functional groups. Gasoline primarily consists of a variety of hydrocarbons, particularly alkanes (also known as paraffins). Alkanes have the general formula CnH2n+2 and can be subdivided into the following three groups: the linear straight-chain alkanes, branched alkanes, and cycloalkanes.

Common Alkenes in Gasoline

› Ethylene (C2H4)

Ethylene is the simplest alkene and may be present in trace amounts. It is lighter than air and is easily ignited and a flame can easily flash back to the source of the leak. It is primarily used in the production of plastics but can also be found as a minor component in some gasoline blends.

› Propylene (C3H6)

Propylene is a colorless gas with a faint petroleum like odor. It is shipped as a liquefied gas under its own vapor pressure. Like ethylene, is primarily produced for industrial purposes (e.g., in plastics) but can also appear in gasoline.

› Butylene (C4H8)

Butylene exists in various isomeric forms (1-butene, cis-2-butene, trans-2-butene) and may be found in gasoline as well.

› 1-Pentene (C5H10)

This alkene is a linear chain compound and can also be present in gasoline.

Alkynes

Alkynes (or acetylenes) are hydrocarbons that contain at least one carbon-carbon triple bond. However, alkynes are generally not found in significant quantities in gasoline.


Why Alkynes are Rare in Gasoline?

› Chemical Instability

Alkynes are highly reactive, which makes them less desirable for use in fuels that need to be stable over time.

› Low Energy Efficiency

While alkynes have high combustion energy, they tend to combust unevenly, potentially causing engine knocking and reducing engine efficiency.

› Production Process

During the refining of crude oil, processes such as catalytic cracking and reforming favor the formation of alkanes, alkenes, and aromatics, not alkynes.

Aromatics Hydrocarbons

Aromatic Hydrocarbons are circularly structured organic compounds that contain sigma bonds along with delocalized pi electrons. They are also referred to as arenes or aryl hydrocarbons. They are an essential component of gasoline, contributing significantly to its octane rating and overall performance. These compounds contain one or more benzene rings in their molecular structure, giving them unique chemical properties. While aromatics improve fuel efficiency, they also have environmental and health risks.

Common Aromatic Hydrocarbons in Gasoline

› Benzene (C6H6)

A volatile and flammable aromatic hydrocarbon. It evaporates into the air very quickly and dissolves slightly in water. It is highly flammable and is formed from both natural processes and human activities Although it boosts octane rating, benzene levels in gasoline are regulated because it is carcinogenic.

› Toluene (C7H8)

Toluene is a clear, colorless liquid with a distinctive smell. Toluene occurs naturally in crude oil and in the tolu tree. It is also produced in the process of making gasoline and other fuels from crude oil and making coke from coal Also known as methylbenzene, it provides a high octane rating and helps reduce engine knocking.

› Xylenes (C8H10)

Includes ortho-, meta-, and para-xylene isomers, used to improve fuel stability and octane rating.and these three different forms are referred to as isomers. Xylene is a colorless, sweet-smelling liquid that catches on fire easily. It occurs naturally in petroleum and coal tar.

› Ethylbenzene (C8H10)

Ethylbenzene is a colorless, flammable liquid that smells like gasoline. It is found in natural products such as coal tar and petroleum and is also found in manufactured products such as inks, insecticides, and paints. Ethylbenzene is used primarily to make another chemical, styrene. Other uses include as a solvent, in fuels, and to make other chemicals. It is also found in small amounts in gasoline and contributes to its volatility
