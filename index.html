Introduction to Gasoline

Definition:

Gasoline, mixture of volatile, flammable liquid hydrocarbons derived from petroleum and used as fuel for internal-combustion engines. It is also used as a solvent for oils and fats. Originally a by-product of the petroleum industry (kerosene being the principal product), gasoline became the preferred automobile fuel because of its high energy of combustion and capacity to mix readily with air in a carburetor.

Importance to society

› Economy

Factories and production plants depend on energy to operate machinery and processes. Without fuel, the production of goods and services would come to a halt, affecting the economy and the availability of essential products.

› Trade

Gasoline plays a crucial role in facilitating trade globally. As a primary fuel source for transportation, it enables the movement of goods and services across vast distances, enhancing supply chain efficiency. The availability of gasoline influences trade patterns, particularly in countries reliant on road transport for both domestic and international trade.

› Transportation

Fuel is at the heart of modern transportation. Personal vehicles, buses, trucks and airplanes depend on fossil fuels to operate. Without a steady supply of gasoline and diesel, mobility would be severely affected, impacting logistics, commerce and people’s daily lives.

› Overall

the fact that fuel remains the foundation of our modern society demonstrates the great importance of fuel. From mobility and industry to power generation and critical infrastructure, this material is fundamental to the functioning of our daily lives.

Creators of Gasolines

Edwin Drake (1819-1880)

Edwin Dug the first crude oil well in Pennsylvania in 1859 and distilled the oil to produce kerosene for lighting. Although other petroleum products, including gasoline, were also produced in the distillation process, Drake had no use for the gasoline and other products, so he discarded them. It wasn't until 1892, with the invention of the automobile, that gasoline was recognized as a valuable fuel. By 1920, 9 million vehicles powered by gasoline were on the road, and service stations selling gasoline were opening around the country. Today, gasoline is the fuel for nearly all light-duty vehicles in the United States.


Vladimir Nikolayevich Ipatieff (1867-1952)

Vladimir Ipatieff was a notable Russian-American chemist recognized for his significant contributions to the field of petroleum refining. He is best known for developing the method of catalytic cracking, which transformed the production of gasoline and other fuels. This process allows heavier fractions of crude oil to be converted into lighter, more valuable products, including gasoline, making it more efficient to produce high-octane fuels necessary for the growing automotive industry​.


William Merriam Burton

Burton was an American chemist who developed a widely used thermal cracking process for crude oil. Burton was born in Cleveland, Ohio. In 1886, he received a Bachelor of Science degree at Western Reserve University. He earned a PhD at Johns Hopkins University in 1889.
